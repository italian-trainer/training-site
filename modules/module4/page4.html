Professional Service

  1. What are the guests’ needs?
    • Some guests, especially at lunch, may be in more of a hurry than others so you wouldn’t want to hold those entrées for very long, if even at all.
  2. How long will the items take to prepare?
    • Every item has a different preparation time. You may need to consider how long both the appetizers and the entrées generally take to reach the table.
    • A special note on preparation time: all baked dishes, including pizza, take a minimum of 20 minutes once the kitchen receives the ticket for them. So, entrée courses that include a baked dish probably don’t ever need to be held.
  3. How busy is the kitchen?
    • A busy kitchen will obviously take a little longer to get a ticket prepared and out once they receive it, so you may want to send your second course a little earlier, so it gets into the line sooner.

Learning to pace your tables and time the courses appropriately will come with experience and practice. You’ll get plenty of chances to see your trainers’ timing in action, so make sure you pick their brains about the how and why.

To start with, talk through the following scenarios with your trainer and explain to them how you would time them:

  1. First Course: Two side Caesar salads
    Second Course: Chicken Piccata and Meat Ravioli
  2. First Course: Split full Cleopatra Salad
    Second Course: Chicken Parmigiana and Shrimp Maria (at lunch)
  3. First Course: Meatballs and Calamari
    Second Course: Boulevard Pizza to share
  4. First Course: Bruschetta to share
    Second Course: Pasta Carbonara and Mushroom Risotto
  5. First Course: Minestrone Soup and Formaggio Fritti
    Second Course: Vegetarian Lasagna and Clams with Linguine
